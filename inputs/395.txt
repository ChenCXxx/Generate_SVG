Introduction


\label{sec: intro}
Large language models (LLMs) with the autoregressive paradigm have gained remarkable performance across various tasks due to their potential reasoning ability \cite{llmsfewshot-iclr20, llmssolve-arxiv22}. The guarantee of such ability in complex tasks heavily relies on chain-of-thought (CoT) \cite{cot-nips22} prompting, which provides step-by-step reasoning examples. This approach suggests that the reasoning ability can be elicited through a chain of thoughts, where a thought serves as an intermediate step toward problem solving.

Thus, subsequent studies \cite{complexity-arxiv22, self-consistency-arxiv22,tot-arxiv23,graphthought-arxiv23}, especially Tree-of-Thought (ToT) \cite{tot-arxiv23}, have been proposed to improve CoT. To guarantee effectiveness, the prompt of these approaches generally includes human annotations on one specific task. Such a reliance limits their scalability. Recent work that either employs a double-check with LLMs to improve answers \cite{refiner-arxiv23,self-verification-arxiv23,Self-refine} or boosts prompts based on feedback \cite{php-arxiv23,prefer-arxiv23,promptboosting-icml23,BPE-arxiv23} has demonstrated significant promise. The existing literature generally tends to discard ineffective thoughts from the prompt. However, humans typically can continuously learn from errors by carefully analyzing them to gain experience, thereby gradually improving performance. We therefore ask: whether the thought generation of LLMs can dispense with human annotation and mimic such a problem-solving way of humans to achieve effective reasoning across various tasks?

The \textit{experience} specifically contains the thought chain itself, the corresponding error reports, and detailed advice on revising each reasoning step. Thus, those ineffective thoughts marked with a red cross can also contribute to prompt refinement. By accumulating experiences over iterations in the prompt, BoT can eventually yield a correct thought chain starting from a simple prompt. The examples presented here are extracted from results obtained by applying GPT-4 with BoT on the Game of 24 task.}

This paper proposes a novel framework, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:botintro}, referred to as the Boosting of Thoughts (BoT), which achieves the \textit{boosting} mechanism that embraces \textit{aggregation} and \textit{experience}, thereby enabling the progressive refinement of unreliable reasoning steps (weak thoughts) by learning from errors to eventually solve various problems. Starting with a simple prompt without human annotations for LLMs, BoT may get weak thoughts. With \textit{aggregation}, BoT is capable of deriving a more logical and effective thought chain from them, thereby guiding the subsequent refinement. This guidance in our framework is achieved by tuning the prompt with \textit{experience}, which is the detailed error reports, advice, and instructions of each reasoning step obtained by exploiting LLMs to analyze the aggregated chain. When such \textit{experience} accumulates in the prompt, it gradually leads to stronger thoughts. 

Specifically, BoT implements such a Boosting mechanism as an \textit{experience}-driven iteration process, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:botintro}. In each iteration, for a given prompt, BoT builds massive simplistic thought structures in parallel with the LLM. We select the tree structure as in ToT \cite{tot-arxiv23} but significantly modify it to weighted binary trees with various growth strategies for our boosting purposes. After extracting the root-to-leaf branch with the highest score per tree, the aggregation component of BoT is performed to aggregate them into one single thought chain. Subsequently, this chain is evaluated by the same LLM to gain the \textit{experience}, which is added to the prompt as guidance for the thought generation in the next iteration. 

Our contributions can be summarized in three folds. First, instead of generating more complicated structures for thoughts with well-designed prompts, this paper shows that it is possible to rely solely on a simple initial prompt, as weak thoughts can be refined progressively based on previous experience toward solving problems. Second, to achieve such a boosting mechanism, we propose Boosting of Thoughts (BoT), a novel framework that performs an \textit{experience}-driven iterative process. Due to starting from a simple prompt, BoT is scalable across various tasks. While guaranteeing effectiveness, BoT is fast as it builds simplistic thought structures in parallel and converges to a solution after a few iterations. Finally, with GPT-4 and LlamaV2, we evaluate the performance of BoT on complex mathematical problems. Finally, relying on GPT-4 \cite{gpt4-arxiv23} and LlamaV2 \cite{llama2-arxiv23}, we evaluate the performance of BoT on complex mathematical problems. The problem-solving rates indicate that BoT, employing binary tree thought structures, significantly surpasses the current state-of-the-art on the \texttt{GSM8K} and \texttt{AQuA} while achieving the second-best results on other datasets. Especially on the new challenging task, Game of 24 \cite{tot-arxiv23}, BoT is $9.7\%$ higher than the leading approach ToT. Our BoT thus demonstrates that, through enhancing the prompt by accumulating error analysis of ineffective thought chains and the corresponding advice, even without human annotation, LLMs are scalable across various tasks while sustaining high performance.


Related Work
\label{sec: relatedwork}

\textbf{Multi-Step Reasoning}. The prominent work Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting \cite{cot-nips22} shows that step-by-step reasoning behaviors from LLMs can be elicited by providing intermediate reasoning steps, termed thoughts, within the prompt for each question, as also supported by Self-Consistency \cite{self-consistency-arxiv22} and a series of CoT-based work \cite{least2most-arxiv22,complexity-arxiv22}. The recent work, Tree of Thoughts (ToT) \cite{tot-arxiv23}, converts the sequential reasoning process into a tree structure, in which each thought (node) may consider previous reasoning paths to produce multiple next-step thoughts. With such backtracking and expanded exploration during reasoning, ToT performs well on problems that even challenge GPT-4 \cite{gpt4-arxiv23}. Considering its high ability, the base thought structure of BoT largely utilizes this tree thought structure ToT. And, thanks to the boosting framework, the tree structure generated in each iteration of BoT is binary and shallow instead of the ToT's complex tree, in which each node corresponds to massive child nodes. However, the base structure is not restricted to ToT. In contrast, BoT is flexible as the base thought structure can be either ToT, GoT \cite{graphthought-arxiv23}, or CR \cite{CR-arxiv23}, where Graph of Thoughts (GoT) \cite{graphthought-arxiv23} is the most recent work that expands the thought structure into a graph format. This paper will only focus on the ToT as the base thought structure and leave the usage of GoT for future work. 

\textbf{Automatic Prompting}. Releasing humans from task-specific prompts attracts much attention \cite{autoprompt-arxiv20}. To guarantee the reasoning ability of LLMs, conventional CoT \cite{cot-nips22} relies on human priors to manually generate task-specific demonstrations as the prompt. However, the zero-shot CoT \cite{llm-zeroshot-arxiv22} shows that even without hand-crafted examples, by simply adding ``Let's think step by step'' to the prompt, LLMs are able to perform step-by-step reasoning toward accurate answers. These insights have spurred a series of subsequent studies. Auto-CoT \cite{automic-chain-arxiv22} eliminates manual efforts by retrieving usable reasoning chains generated by zero-shot CoT. Active-Prompt \cite{activeprompt-arxiv23} first measures the uncertainty of a set of questions and thus selects only the uncertain ones to be annotated by humans. ToT \cite{tot-arxiv23} can also reduce manual efforts, but for each task, it still requires experts to provide possible next-step thoughts in the prompt. Our paper introduces a novel boosting approach for manual-free prompting. Starting with a simple prompt, BoT iteratively enhances it based on the analysis of LLMs on thoughts.

\textbf{Prompt Engineering via Feedback}. Utilizing responses from LLMs to the input prompt as feedback for further prompt revisions has garnered much attention. Those who continuously revise the given prompt based on evaluation descriptions from LLMs aim to gain an accurate answer \cite{self-verification-arxiv23}. Using a similar higher-level idea of our paper, SELF-REFINE \cite{Self-refine} proposes an iterative self-refinement algorithm to let the LLM produce feedback for its output for further refinement. PHP \cite{php-arxiv23} simplifies this process by directly adding a solution from the previous answer as a hint to the subsequent prompt. REFINER \cite{refiner-arxiv23} is also related to our paper as it evaluates each reasoning step as feedback to produce a more reasonable one. Another line of research explores ensembles, particularly leveraging the boosting mechanism \cite{adaboost-icml1996} to refine the prompt using feedback from a set of examples. They adjust the prompt to focus on the unsolved problems in the previous iteration by either adding a few shot examples uncertain in the previous \cite{BPE-arxiv23} or relying on a feedback-reflect-refine process \cite{prefer-arxiv23}. APO \cite{APO-arxiv23} iteratively refines a prompt, using the performance of the prior prompt to form a natural language for optimization. These works prove the effectiveness of the boosting mechanism in prompt engineering. However, our work is the first to highlight the importance of error analysis in enhancing the prompt toward generating effective reasoning chains. The proposed BoT extends this insight to implement an automated prompting framework by iteratively accumulating an ensemble of trial-and-error reasoning experiences in the prompt. 



Boosting of Thoughts
\label{sec: method}

\subsection{Background}

The objective of prompt engineering is to design a prompt $\sI$ containing multiple language sequences, such that with this prompt as input, a pre-trained large language model (LLM) denoted as $p_{\theta}$ parameterized by $\theta$, can obtain the desired language sequence $y$. Thus, the standard Input-Output (IO) can be formulated as $y \sim p_{\theta}\left(y|\sI\left(X, Q\right)\right)$ in which $\sI\left(\cdot\right)$ means that the prompt wraps task instructions $X$ and the corresponding question $Q$. 

The prompt can be designed in a more delicate way to guide the LLM toward solving a problem in a step-by-step manner. Each intermediate reasoning step is denoted as $z_i$ (a.k.a \textit{thought}). CoT \cite{cot-nips22} provides few-shot examples with the answer of each example containing a chain of \textit{thought} $z_{1...n}$. This leads to $y \sim p_{\theta}\left(y|\sI\left(\left[z_{1...n}\right]^N, X, Q\right)\right)$ where $N$ is the number of examples included in the prompt.

Instead of pre-preparing examples in the prompt, a more adaptive way is to design prompts to guide the LLMs to gradually generate the thought $z_i$ during the reasoning process. This can be formalized as $z_i \sim p_{\theta}\left(z_i|\sI\left(z_{1...{i-1}}, X, Q\right)\right)$. Finally, the solution is formalized as $y \sim p_{\theta}\left(y|\sI\left(z_{1...n}, X, Q\right)\right)$. The representative approach, ToT \cite{tot-arxiv23}, further extends this sequential reasoning steps into a tree structure in which $C$ next-step thoughts can be generated. Thus, the \textit{thought strcuture} can be chain or tree.



\subsection{Framework}

\begin{figure*}[t!]

With the simple prompt $\sI^t$ as input, The Thought Structures Generation (Stage 1) outputs massive heterogenous tree thought structures. Thought Structures Aggregation (Stage 2) aggregated them into a thought chain $\overline{z}_{1...n}$, which is analyzed in Stage 3 to produce \textit{experience} to further enhance the prompt.}

Existing literature that aims to generate the prompt with correct CoT examples or design delicate thought generation structures has three limitations. First, ineffective thoughts in those approaches are generally ignored or discarded. However, a human, who is not an expert in one field, particularly relies on analyzing previous errors to collect more experience to perform correctly on the next try. Second, they are less scalable because, for each task, an example of generating the next thoughts, such as $\sI\left(z_{1}|z_{0}, X, Q\right)$, should be provided in the prompt. Finally, the thought structure, such as the tree \cite{tot-arxiv23}, is generated to be overly complex to explore more reasoning steps for a better solution. This is largely due to the obtained solution may not be further revised.

In this paper, we argue that the prompt can be enhanced by continuously collecting the analysis of LLMs on those ineffective thoughts -- wrong reasoning steps in a chain of thought. Therefore, even a simple prompt, such as $\sI\left(X, Q\right)$, potentially leading to ineffective thoughts, can be progressively refined by relying on such analysis to gain powerful thoughts toward the solution. 

We propose Boosting of Thoughts (BoT), an automated prompting framework incorporating, which achieves prompt \textit{boosting} with an \textit{experience}-driven iteration process commencing with a \textbf{simple prompt}. As summarized in Fig.~\ref{fig: bot}, each iteration $t$ of BoT includes three stages. TThe Thought Structures Generation stage is able to fully explore reasoning chains generated by LLMs with the input prompt $\sI^t$. In the second stage, these thought structures are aggregated to form a reasoning chain, which is to be analyzed by LLMs in the third stage to produce feedback containing error reports and detailed revision advice. Combining the aggregated reasoning chain with the feedback results in a new \textit{experience}, denoted as $\mathbf{F}^t$. Thus, the prompt is enhanced by accumulating these experiences $\mathbf{F}^{1...t}$ over iterations.

\textbf{Simple Prompt}. For any task, in iteration $t=0$, we create a simple initial prompt $\sI^0\left(S, X, Q, \mathbf{F}^0, \left\{G_i\right\}\right)$, where $S$ represents task-agnostic descriptions while the terms $X$ and $Q$ respectively denote the task information and the question. The \textit{experience} part of the prompt is denoted as $\mathbf{F}^0$, which should be empty at the beginning. $\left\{G_i\right\}$ is a placeholder that is waiting to be filled during building thought structures. In other words, when generating the next thought $z_i$, $\left\{G_i\right\}$ will be substituted with the preceding chain of thoughts $z_{1..,i-1}$. 

\textbf{Thought Structures Generation}. After collecing \textit{experience} $\mathbf{F}^{t-1}$, the prompt in the iteration $t$ can be $\sI^{t}\left(S, X, Q, \mathbf{F}^{1,...,t-1}, \left\{G_i\right\}\right)$. Based on this prompt, BoT generates $M$ thought structures in parallel. BoT is inherently capable of embracing any thought structure, such as the chain \cite{cot-nips22} or tree \cite{tot-arxiv23} structure. Considering the exploration of reasoning steps and experimental results, we investigate the tree thought structure. However, BoT introduces two novel modifications to make it better suited for the boosting framework.

\begin{itemize}
    
\item \textbf{Weighted Binary Tree}. With a simple prompt in each round, BoT builds the weak thoughts structured in low complexity as they can be further revised in the boosting mechanism. Thus, each thought structure of BoT is a shallow weighted binary tree. For simplicity, we retain the notation $z_{1...i-1}$ to represent the thoughts from the root to the parent of node $i$. In addition to providing each node $i$ with one thought $z_i$ and its thought evaluation score $V_i \sim p_{\theta}\left(z_{1...i}, \sI_a, X, Q\right)$,  we incorporate the edge score $V_{i-1, i} \sim p_{\theta}\left(z_{i-1}, z_{i}, \sI_e, X, Q\right)$ between a child node and its parent node, where $\sI_a$ and $\sI_e$ refer to the instructional descriptions for thought and edge evaluations. $V_{i-1, i}$ represents the LLMs' confidence level in generating this reasoning step. Thus, the next thought generation of BoT in this tree structure is formalized as $p_{\theta}\left(z_i|\left(V_{i-1, i}, V_i, \sI^t, X, Q\right)\right)$.

\item \textbf{Tree Heterogeneity}. Unlike ToT \cite{tot-arxiv23}, which seeks to search for a solution in one large and complex tree, BoT aims to build highly heterogeneous tree thought structures. As a result, complete reasoning chains with various logical in trees of BoT are subsequently assessed as \textit{experience}. Therefore, to increase heterogeneity, thought structure generation embraces different tree growth strategies, such as level-wise growth and leaf-wise growth. The former emphasizes exploration but less exploitation \cite{xgboost-kdd17}, while the latter does the opposite \cite{lightgbm-nips17}. Thus, the leaf-wise strategy tends to continue reasoning from the current best thought to reach a better final thought as compared to level-wise growth, but it also tends to get monotonous reasoning chains. Besides, different temperature and Top\_p settings of LLMs are applied. Finally, we use a small max\_depth value in BoT and label a node as a leaf when its $V_{i-1, i}$ and $V_i$ values are outside the specified range $\left[0.3, 0.8\right]$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Thought Structures Aggregation}. Upon obtaining $M$ thought structures, BoT aggregates them into one thought chain denoted as $\overline{z}_{1...n}$. To achieve this, for each thought structure with index $m$, BoT first selects the chain with the highest evaluation score as $z^m_{1...n_m} := \argmax_{z_{1...n}\in {\ermZ}^m} \sum_{i=1}^{n} V_i+V_{i-1,i}$ where ${\ermZ}^m$ denotes the set of all thought chains of $m$-th tree. Subsequently, two strategies exist to obtain $\overline{z}_{1...n}$. 

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Best-First Aggregation}. BoT relies on $\argmax_{z_{1...n}\in \left\{\ermZ^m\right\}_{m=1}^M} \sum_{i=1}^nV_i+V_{i-1,i}$ to choose the best one as $\overline{z}_{1...n}$ from $M$ thought structures. This algorithm is fast but may lead to an unreasonable chain that is hard to guide the following refinement. 

\item \textbf{Greedy Aggregation}. BoT is allowed to perform a greedy search on $\left\{\ermZ^m\right\}_{m=1}^M$ to assemble a new thought chain that may not exist and is globally optimal. Starting from the initial thought, generally the root node of the tree, BoT obtains $\overline{z}_{1} = \argmax_{z_j \in \left\{z^m_{1}\right\}_{m=1}^M}V_j+V_{j-1,j}$. Subsequently, to obtain $\overline{z}_i$ for $\overline{z}_{i-1}$, BoT searches all thoughts where the previous step is $\overline{z}_{i-1}$ in $\left\{\ermZ^m\right\}_{m=1}^M$. 
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Thought Chain Analysis}. To gain insights into what should be adjusted to enhance the prompt to generate better thoughts, BoT utilizes the self-evaluation ability \cite{self-verification-arxiv23} of LLMs to assess $\overline{z}_{1...n}$. Specifically, with the prompt $\sI_f^t\left(\overline{z}_{1...n}, X, Q\right)$ as input, LLM outputs a feedback paragraph containing issues report of this thought chain $\overline{z}_{1...n}$ and detailed advice. This feedback will be added to $\mathbf{F}^{1, ..., t-1}$ as a new \textit{experience} in thought generation, resulting $\mathbf{F}^{1, ..., t}$.

\textbf{Iterative Refinement}. Through boosting mechanism, $\mathbf{F}^{1,...,t}$ is employed to iteratively enhance the prompt, resulting in $\sI^{t+1}\left(S, X, Q, \mathbf{F}^{1,...,t}, \left\{G_i\right\}\right)$ for the $(t+1)$-th iteration. As the iterations progress, $\mathbf{F}^{1,...,t}$ may encompass many typical, unreasonable thought chains alongside those closer to a solution, all with well-defined analysis outcomes. Therefore, even when starting with a simple prompt, BoT iteratively refines this prompt to produce the correct reasoning steps leading to the accurate solution. After $T$ iterations, we utilize the $\sI^{t+1}$ as input prompt for the LLM to gain the final answer. 


Experiments
\label{sec: exp}

\textbf{Datasets}. Experiments are performed on benchmark datasets with diverse mathematical problems, including \texttt{MMLU} \cite{mmlu-iclr21}, \texttt{SVAMP}  \cite{SVAMP-arxiv21}, \texttt{GSM8K} \cite{verifier-arxiv21}, \texttt{AQuA} \cite{AQuA-arxiv17} and \texttt{MATH} \cite{MATH-arxiv21}. Besides, we include a challenging mathematical reasoning task, Game of 24 \cite{tot-arxiv23}, where the goal is to use four numbers and basic arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division) to obtain 24 in $1$ equation. Thus, the solution includes $3$ intermediate steps.

\textbf{Competitors}. Apart from the benchmark approach, standard Input-output (IO), the comparison approaches include Chain-of-thought (CoT) \cite{cot-nips22}, CoT-SC \cite{self-consistency-arxiv22} and Complex CoT \cite{complexity-arxiv22}, in which the input prompt contains a few-shot examples ($8$) with human annotations. Also, BoT is also compared with related works, such as Tree of thoughts (ToT) \cite{tot-arxiv23} with the breadth limit $5$, Progressive-Hint Prompting (PHP) \cite{php-arxiv23}, and the state-of-the-art CSV \cite{csv-arxiv23}.

\textbf{Large Language Models}. We conduct experiments on the two most recent models: GPT-4 \cite{gpt4-arxiv23} and Llama2 \cite{llama2-arxiv23}. GPT-4 is accessed via OpenAI APIs, while the llama-2-13b-chat model is downloaded from MetaAI to perform experiments locally. To construct the heterogeneous tree thought structures, BoT randomly chooses the temperature from the range of $\left[0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.7, 0.9, 1.1, 1.5\right]$ and the top\_p from the range of $\left[0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9\right]$.

\textbf{Settings}. If not explicitly stated, BoT, in all experiments, performs $T=10$ iterations of running and builds $M=15$ thought structures, each being a weighted binary tree because this tends to achieve optimal results. Besides, for those benchmark datasets, we set the depth of the tree to be $5$ while the corresponding depth in Game of 24 is $3$. BoT+CoT means our simple prompt includes $5$ examples from CoT \cite{cot-nips22}. In the ablation study, when no \textit{experience} is accumulated in BoT, $8$ examples of CoT will be provided in the prompt. 

\textbf{Metrics}. All experiments report the Solve Rate (\%) of the task as the evaluation results. To extract target answers from the output $\overline{z}^T_{1...n}$ of BoT, we specifically set the formatted description of the answer for LLMs. For commonly used datasets, the desired answer format is ``The answer is: .'' For the Game of 24, we utilize ``Step idx, Current set: , Selected two numbers: , Operation: , Computed new number: , Remaining numbers: , New set: ''. Thus, we directly compare the ground truth with the number presented in the new set. Following ToT \cite{tot-arxiv23}, we report the Solving Rate across $100$ hard games as the metric.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{subsec:main}
The primary experimental results are summarized in Table.~\ref{table:maincomparision} and Fig.~\ref{fig:gpt4llama2}, where we present insights into the overall performance of BoT. Our findings indicate that the proposed BoT with Boosting mechanism 1). obtains competitive problem-solving rates in most datasets without human annotations; 2). is capable of reaching a new state-of-the-art on \texttt{GSM8K} and \texttt{AQuA} when provided with CoT examples. However, experimental results also demonstrate that BoT heavily relies on \textit{experience}, thus is sensitive to the ability of LLMs.

Specifically, in Table.~\ref{table:maincomparision}, BoT, starting from a simple initial prompt and performing basic chatting, eventually obtains a \texttt{GSM8K} solve rate $0.1\%$ higher than the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) CSV \cite{csv-arxiv23}, which heavily relies on code interpreter of GPT-4. Considering \texttt{AQuA}, BoT is $2.5\%$ higher than SOTA. This demonstrates that by adding error analysis and advice to the prompt without human annotations, LLMs are able to perform well on complex reasoning. The main reason is that a simple prompt can be iteratively refined by accumulating prior \textit{experience} towards accurate problem-solving. After including CoT examples in the prompt, BoT+CoT outperforms SOTA by $1.3\%$ on average in \texttt{GSM8K} and \texttt{AQuA} datasets. We argue that the CoT examples can be regarded as the success cases in the \textit{experience}, directly guiding the subsequent thought structures generation of BoT. Thus, cooperating with the iteration refinement, BoT+CoT reaches a new SOTA. It also deserves to show that because BoT can gradually collect analysis of various reasoning chains (bad or good) as \textit{experience}, it is consistently close to the BoT+CoT. However, BoT and BoT+CoT, especially BoT, are at least $18\%$ lower than SOTA in \texttt{MATH}. This observation means weak LLMs may not perform well with BoT due to their lower ability to analyze reasoning chains for an effective \textit{experience}, as supported by Fig.~\ref{fig:gpt4llama2}.

Fig.~\ref{fig:gpt4llama2} presents that with BoT, GPT-4 and Llama2 are respectively improved by $11.6\%$ and $4.4\%$ on average in three datasets. The two numbers show a clear trend that when the LLM is weaker, BoT's performance drops significantly. With powerful GPT-4, as presented in Fig.~\ref{fig:gpt4llama2}, BoT and BoT-CoT behave similarly to those shown in Table.~\ref{table:maincomparision}. Additionally, their performance escalates along a similar trend as the number of trees varies from $1$ to $20$. As Llama2 is weaker, BoT is unable to benefit from its analysis to perform the \textit{experience}-driven iteration process, which is particularly shown by Fig.~\ref{fig:gpt4llama2} (a). When provided with valid success cases, i.e., 5-shots, BoT, through progressive refinement, can still help Llama2 to solve more problems than the baseline even though the improvement is limited.
